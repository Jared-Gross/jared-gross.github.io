Introduction

What inspired you to become a teacher?

I was fortunate to have many inspiring and supportive teachers who guided me throughout my education. Their encouragement played a crucial role in my decision to pursue teaching as a career. Additionally, I believe I possess a unique and valuable skill set that can be used to educate and inspire the next generation.

What are your core values and beliefs about education?

I believe in a personalized approach to teaching. Teachers should invest time in listening and engaging with students, both in and out of the classroom. Even a brief conversation in the hallway can make a difference.

Building positive relationships is crucial for student success. Teaching students how to connect socially and communicate effectively is a lifelong skill that will benefit them in various aspects of life. Additionally, fostering critical thinking and problem-solving skills is essential. Encouraging students to verbalize their thought processes and engage in meaningful discussions is a key part of my teaching philosophy.

How do you see your role in the classroom and in the school community?

I aim to teach high school-level mathematics, science, and English or social studies in my Hutterian community. My strong background in computer science and robotics makes me passionate about integrating these subjects alongside core curriculum requirements to create a cross-curricular learning environment.

What strengths do you bring to the teaching profession?

My background in computer science and programming provides me with strong problem-solving and critical-thinking skills. These abilities are transferable to various subjects and will be valuable for students to experience firsthand. Additionally, I am highly structured and organized, which aligns well with the responsibilities of a teacher.

Experience with Children and Adolescents

What past experiences have shaped your understanding of working with young people?

Before starting university, I spent a year assisting in various classrooms, which allowed me to experience working with students across all grade levels. This experience helped me determine my interest in teaching and the age group I would prefer to work with.

During my first practicum at Brennan School, I worked under Elma Maendel, my mentor teacher. While I was assessed on teaching Kindergarten, I also gained experience teaching students up to Grade 9.

What grade levels and subjects have you worked with so far?

I have worked with all grades from Kindergarten to Grade 12.

How have these experiences influenced your teaching philosophy?

My initial interest in teaching was fueled by the joy of working with young learners and witnessing their enthusiasm for learning. However, as I pursued my undergraduate degree in Mathematics, I gradually shifted my focus to high school education.

Teaching at Brennan School helped me realize that Early Years teaching was not the best fit for me, despite my training. I felt a strong desire to challenge myself and my students with more advanced topics.

Successes and Challenges in my First Practicum

Key Moments During my Practicum

One of my most significant growth areas was developing my teacher voice.

Additionally, my faculty supervisor noted that I demonstrated strong lesson planning, organization, and adaptability, especially in multi-grade classrooms.

Challenges and How I Navigated Them

One of my biggest challenges was a lack of experience with school policies and strategies.

However, with time and practice, I learned how to better manage classroom transitions and lesson closures.

Teaching Goals and Aspirations

What grade levels or subjects do you see yourself teaching in the future? Why?

I would prefer to teach Grades 8–12, particularly in Computer Science. My strong background in this field allows me to offer students a unique perspective on problem-solving and critical thinking, both of which are essential life skills.

One of my most impactful educational experiences was having a teacher who conducted a weekly virtues class. This approach resonated with me, and I would love to incorporate a similar character education program into my teaching.

Additionally, I have noticed that many people mislabel their emotions, which prevents them from managing their feelings effectively. Teaching students how to navigate emotions in a healthy and constructive manner is something I believe is crucial.

What teaching methods or approaches do you feel most comfortable using?

I am structured, organized, and punctual. I value details and ensure that students fully understand concepts rather than leaving them to figure things out on their own.

What are some innovative strategies you’d like to try in your future classroom?

I would like to implement a more ungraded approach, where I engage in conversations with students about their progress. By doing this, I can gain a deeper understanding of their learning journey.

Additionally, I believe formative and summative assessments should be balanced. Formative assessments should challenge students’ understanding rather than simply testing their recall of information.

Contributions Beyond the Classroom

How do you see yourself contributing to extracurricular activities?

I would like to establish a makerspace in my classroom, equipped with 3D printing, robotics, and electronics.

Additionally, I am passionate about teaching social and emotional intelligence. In today’s digital-driven world, these skills are often overlooked but remain essential.

How do your hobbies and interests support student engagement?

My background in computer science allows me to introduce unique approaches to problem-solving and critical thinking.

Furthermore, I am interested in 3D printing and modeling, which would provide students with opportunities to develop creative solutions to real-world problems.

Evolving Teaching Philosophy

I aspire to be a calm, confident, and professional teacher who fosters critical and creative thinking following a growth-mindset while prioritizing student-centered learning.

Self-Awareness & Critical Reflection

What are some beliefs about teaching that you are beginning to question?

I am mindful of the "curse of knowledge"—the assumption that what I know is common knowledge. I believe that by applying a structured, problem-solving-oriented approach, I can mitigate this bias.

Additionally, I believe teachers should only assess what they explicitly teach. There is no reason to include questions on an assessment that have not been thoroughly covered in class.

How do your personal experiences shape your classroom interactions?

I take a professional, punctual, and articulate approach in both digital and real-life interactions.

As I plan to teach within a Hutterite community, many students will already know me. Thus, modeling professionalism and clear communication is crucial.

Books That Shaped My Educational Approach

One of the most insightful books I have read is Positive Discipline for Children with Special Needs by Arlene Raphael, Steven Foster, and Jane Nelsen. It provided me with a structured, respectful, and effective approach to helping students develop responsibility and resilience. This book reinforced my belief in fostering an inclusive and empathetic classroom environment.

Areas for Growth

I am to:

  • Refine how I close lessons to ensure students leave with a clear understanding of key concepts.
  • Explore professional development opportunities that focus on effective teaching methods for specific topics, allowing me to enhance my instructional techniques.
  • Attend workshops and stay engaged in professional learning communities to continue evolving as an educator.
  • Focus on improving my classroom management skills, particularly in handling disruptions and guiding students more effectively.

Fostering a Growth Mindset

Fostering a growth mindset is just as important as academic learning. I approach student ideas and thought processes with positivity and respect, guiding them through questioning and conversation rather than simply correcting them. I model lifelong learning by demonstrating curiosity and enthusiasm for acquiring new knowledge, making it clear that learning is an ongoing process, not just something that happens in school.

Continuously Reflecting and Growing as an Educator

To continuously refine my approach, I engage in constant reflection. I stay open to growth, new ideas, and challenges, while also seeking student feedback to ensure they are learning effectively and in ways that resonate with them. Professional development is a priority—I attend and present at workshops, network with other educators, and exchange ideas, challenges, and solutions with colleagues. Teaching is a dynamic profession, and I am committed to evolving alongside it.

Establishing a Student-Centered Learning Structure

My framework for learning begins with understanding where students are at and what they need. To do this, I establish a diagnostic test to assess prior knowledge and then determine what they are interested in learning, how they want to learn, and why. With this foundation, I create a structured plan that incorporates multiple modalities, ensuring that learning is both engaging and aligned with curriculum expectations. My approach is transparent, structured, and organized, providing a clear path for both teaching and learning.

Encouraging Engagement and Active Participation

Engagement and ownership are key components of my teaching. I encourage collaboration by inviting students to the board to answer questions, working through problems in groups or individually while I observe, assist, and facilitate deeper understanding. Inquiry-based learning, active participation, and collaboration are essential—students should explore, question, and engage with material in a way that fosters critical thinking and problem-solving.

Differentiating Instruction to Meet Diverse Needs

To meet diverse needs, I take the time to understand how my students learn best. Differentiation means recognizing their unique learning styles and adapting my teaching accordingly. I believe in being the example—showing, guiding, and supporting students rather than expecting them to grasp something on their own the first time. A structured approach with clear explanations, stories, and analogies helps establish a solid foundation before transitioning into discussions and interactive learning.

Aligning Lessons with Curriculum Standards

Aligning with curriculum standards is crucial. I start with the curriculum and learning outcomes to structure my lessons, providing a clear framework while allowing room for meaningful, real-world learning experiences. My yearly plan ensures that all required outcomes are met while maintaining flexibility to adapt based on student needs and interests.

Providing Meaningful and Constructive Feedback

Feedback is equally important. I provide detailed, constructive feedback while understanding that some students may overlook it, especially if their grade is lower than expected or if they are disengaged. Regardless, it is my professional responsibility to ensure students receive the guidance they need. I also give them opportunities to discuss their progress, ask questions, and improve their work because I believe in second chances.

Using Assessment to Guide Learning

Assessment should guide learning rather than just measure it. I use student-generated self-assessments, such as exit tickets, to shape the next lesson, ensuring we pick up where they left off rather than where I left off. This keeps learning student-centered and responsive. I see assessments as a tool not just for evaluation but for reflection and adjustment in my teaching.

Integrating Technology and Innovation

Technology and innovation play a role in my framework as well. I plan to incorporate subjects like coding, 3D printing, and collaborative online tools as electives, allowing students to solve real-world problems through hands-on application. Encouraging digital literacy and problem-solving is vital, as these skills will benefit students beyond the classroom.
